About

I am a doctoral candidate at MIT Sloan School of Management, affiliated with the System Dynamics group. My research examines how organizations and managers within them shape economic mobility and inequality, with a particular focus on low-wage, hourly work. I am additionally interested in identifying practices that organizational leaders can adopt to support worker mobility without compromising firm performance.

My dissertation examines how frontline managers in warehouse fulfillment centers influence gender inequality in earnings through the hours they allocate to employees. My other projects investigate why gender pay gaps vary across similar workplaces, how strategic initiatives such as DEI practices and education reforms sometimes fail to work as planned, and which employer practices support mobility for non-college and historically marginalized workers.

I use a variety of research methods to answer these questions but primarily leverage dynamic modeling and simulation along with econometric analysis of large-scale administrative data. Prior to joining MIT, I served as a pre-doctoral research fellow at the Channing Division of Network Medicine at Harvard Medical School. I graduated with a B.S. in Systems Science Engineering from Washington University in St. Louis.

Job Market Paper

Managers and the Gender Gap in Hours in Low-Wage Work

Abstract

How do managers shape the gender gap in hours? For low-wage hourly workers, hours are a direct determinant of pay, and yet research on gender inequality in hours has focused on supply-side explanations, such as women’s caregiving constraints. The role of manager’s decisions in the hours that men and women work remains largely unexamined. Frontline managers impact the hours that workers receive because they are often tasked with deploying lean scheduling strategies that attempt to match staffing to fluctuating demand. In this paper, I investigate whether and how managers shape the gender gap in hours, focusing on two managerial characteristics: gender and tenure. I use longitudinal administrative pay data for nearly 9,000 full-time warehouse workers to estimate within-individual models that compare the same worker’s hours across changes in their direct supervisor. This specification allows me to isolate managerial allocation from stable worker preferences. I find that women receive fewer hours than men in the same job, and that this gap is driven primarily by differences in regular hours, or the baseline hours that define full-time work, rather than overtime. Managerial experience substantially shapes this gap. When a woman receives a newly appointed supervisor, her regular hours drop by roughly one hour per week. This effect is concentrated among new male supervisors, who also cut overtime hours for men, though this effect is smaller in magnitude than the penalty for women. The specificity of this effect to new male supervisors suggests that when male supervisors face performance pressure in early tenure, they implement cost-cutting in gendered ways, with women facing the largest penalty to baseline hours that they expect as full-time workers.

Organizational Inequality Regimes: How Hours, Tenure, and Sorting Produce Gender Gaps in US Warehouses

Abstract

How do workplaces shape pay gaps between men and women? Classic theories emphasize that individual characteristics and market conditions determine gender differences in pay. In contrast, theories of the relational drivers of inequality and gendered organizations claim that gender pay gaps are a result of local and workplace-level processes, including managerial decision-making. Despite theoretical attention to local, relational processes, there is little evidence on whether and how workplaces and the actors within them shape gender pay inequality. In this paper, I investigate whether the gender pay gap and the mechanisms producing it vary across similar workplaces within the same firm. I use administrative pay data for nearly 9,000 full-time warehouse workers in 27 fulfillment centers within the same firm to decompose gender pay gaps at the workplace level. I find cross-establishment variation in the size of the gap and in the mechanisms producing it, with hours, tenure, and departmental sorting combining in different ways to advantage or disadvantage women. I interpret this heterogeneity through the lens of Joan Acker’s concept of inequality regimes, or local processes and practices that lead to variations in inequality. In this case, regimes are composed of different patterns of inequality-generating mechanisms. This workplace-level heterogeneity reveals that workplaces with large gender pay gaps tend to be characterized by an uneven distribution of hours, whereas workplaces with smaller or no gaps tend to have a more balanced distribution of hours.

The Dynamics of Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion Practice Adoption

Abstract

Despite the widespread adoption of Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI) initiatives in corporate America, significant disparities persist in the representation, compensation, and treatment of women and racial minorities. This paper investigates why well-intentioned DEI efforts often fail to achieve their intended outcomes and identifies managerial barriers hindering progress. This research employs a qualitative dynamic modeling approach to analyze the complexities of DEI practice implementation within organizations. I conducted a scoping review, focusing on longitudinal and experimental designs to identify key mechanisms influencing the outcomes of DEI practices. The interplay between organizational processes and individual cognitive and behavioral responses can be illustrated via reinforcing and balancing feedback loops that I map onto a causal loop diagram, which reveals how DEI initiatives interact with existing organizational processes and cultural dynamics. This paper introduces a dynamic perspective on DEI practice implementation, highlighting the feedback mechanisms that can either hinder or facilitate progress toward diversity goals. The model reveals that certain DEI practices may inadvertently trigger reinforcing loops that perpetuate inequality. By mapping DEI practices and their effects, this study provides a framework for understanding how DEI outcomes can diverge significantly depending on different implementation strategies. It underscores the importance of considering the endogenous feedback effects of DEI initiatives and offers insights into strategic interventions that can disrupt undesirable reinforcing cycles and promote progress toward organizational diversity, equity, and inclusion.

Publications

Keith, D., Yadama, A. P., O'Neill, E., & Chung, S. (2024). Anticipating the Side Effects of Educational Reform Using System Dynamics Modeling. Review of Research in Education, 48(1), 1–27. DOI

Abstract

Education reform policies have frequently underperformed due to the emergence of side-effects that undermined the intended consequences of the policy interventions. A key reason for this is that linear policy frameworks frequently overlook the multiple and nonlinear feedbacks that exist within complex educational systems. In this paper we introduce System Dynamics (SD) modeling as an analytical framework for anticipating policy side-effects (a.k.a. unintended consequences) ex-ante. We demonstrate the application of SD using the case of declining enrollment in St. Louis Public Schools. Using rich qualitative data from 96 interviews with parents and teachers, we demonstrate how the well-intentioned decisions of stakeholders have led to the system becoming stuck in a capability trap amplified by the well-intentioned decisions of teachers, parents and funders. Our paper highlights how system modeling can be used to anticipate the many consequences of policy interventions over time, positive and negative, guiding improved education policy-making.

Kelly, E., Rahmandad, H., Wilmers, N., & Yadama, A. P. (2023). How Do Employer Practices Affect Economic Mobility? ILR Review, 76(5), 792–832. DOI

Abstract

How can employers facilitate economic mobility for workers, particularly workers of color or those without a college degree? The authors integrate a fragmented literature to assess how employers’ practices affect enhanced economic security and mobility. This article first identifies three pathways linking employers’ practices to mobility: improving material job quality, increasing access to better jobs for historically marginalized workers, and promoting sustainability of employment. The authors provide a critical assessment of the research literature on recruitment and hiring practices; pay and wages; promotion practices; scheduling; leaves; diversity, equity, and inclusion initiatives; and work systems as these practices relate to economic mobility. They then identify strategic questions and feasible designs for enhancing future research on these questions in order to guide policy and management practice.
